## Summary

Chase and a friend were discussing a Y Combinator-inspired idea: build an AI agency that replaces professional services headcount with AI agents, starting with accounting and finance. Through brainstorming, the concept crystallized into a **managed service** model — not SaaS — targeting midsized companies for 1099 processing. The core insight: charge service-level prices ($299–$699/season) while delivering at software-level costs.

A tax domain expert (Will Rosler) reviewed the concept and added critical domain detail: the real complexity in 1099 processing isn't the filing itself — it's getting the correct vendor list in the first place. QBO exports aren't cash-basis-aware, CC payments must be excluded, C-corps don't qualify, and the $600 threshold applies to cash payments only. These upstream cleanup steps are where the AI earns its keep.

The implementation plan is now underway. Plan 1 (Foundation & Client Portal) is fully written: 10 tasks, 20 tests, covering auth, database, QBO OAuth, CSV upload, Stripe checkout, and a provider-agnostic AccountingProvider interface. Plans 2 and 3 cover the AI processing pipeline and W-9 chase/delivery workflows.

## Key Topics

- AI agency as managed service (not SaaS product)
- 1099 processing as the initial service offering and validation wedge
- QBO OAuth + CSV upload with provider-agnostic AccountingProvider interface
- Client journey: Submit → Compile → Chase W-9s → Review → Clarify → File
- Agent model: AI handles ~90% autonomously, human reviewer handles escalations
- Three-tier pricing: $299 Starter / $699 Growth / Enterprise custom
- Tech stack: Next.js 15, Prisma + Neon, Clerk, Stripe, Inngest, Sentry, Resend
- MVP scope with deliberate deferrals (IRS e-filing, state filing, phone outreach)

## Decisions & Insights

- **Managed service, not SaaS** — positions as an agency replacing a firm, not competing with Track1099/Tax1099
- **1099 processing as wedge** — time-boxed, repeatable, high client stress, binary output, clear AI fit
- **Target customer: midsized companies** — have budget to outsource, no full finance team
- **IRS e-filing deferred** — do it manually first to validate demand before automating
- **QBO OAuth added to MVP** — pull raw transactions directly from API; exports aren't cash-basis-aware
- **Provider-agnostic AccountingProvider interface** — QBO + CSV both implement same contract; Xero/FreshBooks added with no downstream changes
- **Inngest for job queue** — durable background functions for AI pipeline steps, no persistent worker needed on Vercel
- **Resend for email** — W-9 outreach, status notifications, contractor copies; free tier covers MVP
- **Sentry for errors** — critical for a financial product with paying clients
- **Upfront payment at submission** — no free processing, clean cash flow, filters uncommitted clients
- **Validation signal**: 5 paying companies complete a full job, 3 say they'd return next year

## Domain Context

From a tax domain expert review (Will Rosler) — the real complexity of 1099 processing.

### Why the vendor list is hard

QuickBooks vendor totals reporting is not cash-basis-aware and doesn't exclude CC payments automatically. Businesses often pay vendors via multiple methods (ACH for most, check or wire for some), making categorization inconsistent. The AI agent must reconcile raw payment data to produce the correct qualifying vendor list — which is the core value, not the filing itself.

**Rules that matter:**

- Required for any vendor paid $600+ on a cash basis in the tax year
- Excluded: C-corps, payments to accounting firms, credit card payments
- Only ACH, wire, and check payments count
- 1099-NEC (contractors), 1099-MISC (rent, attorneys), 1099-INT (non-bank interest)

### The W-9 catch-22

To determine if a vendor needs a 1099, you need their TIN and entity type (from a W-9). But you need to know they qualify first — which requires having their info. Many vendors slip through onboarding without one. Following up near the January deadline is painful and often ignored. If TINs don't match the IRS name/ID on file, filings bounce back.

The agency solves this by proactively chasing missing W-9s via automated email outreach, well before the filing deadline.
